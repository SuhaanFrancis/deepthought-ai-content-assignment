# They fixed the plant. Then every leader told the story for 40 years.

**EXECUTION INSIGHT · ISSUE 01**
*For MDs & CEOs of Indian Mid-Market Companies*

---

It was 11 p.m. in Bloomfield, Connecticut.

George Koenigsaecker walked two Japanese consultants onto the factory floor. The night shift paused. The union was wary. No one quite knew what to expect.

The consultants — students of Taiichi Ohno, architect of the Toyota Production System — didn't speak at first. They just watched. Machines. Movement. Inventory. Flow.

Then one of them asked:

> "It's a big warehouse. Where is your factory?"

The plant belonged to Jacobs Manufacturing Company — "Jake Brake." It made engine brakes for diesel trucks. It was profitable. It had always been profitable.

But underneath, things were breaking.

Quality was inconsistent. Delivery was unreliable. Customers were quietly exploring alternatives. And the same people responsible for hitting today's production targets were also expected to fix tomorrow's problems.

**Production always won.**

The consultant pointed to a key cell on the floor.

"The layout is backward," he said. "Clockwise. Change it to counterclockwise. Each worker uses their right arm — three percent stronger. Three percent productivity gain."

He paused.

> "Would you like to fix it now?"

They worked until 5 a.m.

---

## The Real Problem

### It wasn't effort. It was structure.

Jake Brake didn't have an effort problem.

No one was lazy. No one ignored quality. But the system forced a tradeoff: run today's operations or improve tomorrow's performance. And when those compete, today wins.

**Every time.**

Because today is urgent. Visible. Measured. Improvement is ambiguous. Risky. Easy to postpone.

So improvement plans don't fail loudly. They fade quietly. Quarterly reviews happen. Priorities are aligned. Then Monday morning arrives — and the same constraints reassert. The same fires. The same incentives. Nothing changes.

**You cannot solve a structural problem with individual willpower.**

---

## What Danaher Did Differently

### One person. One mandate. Protected bandwidth.

When Koenigsaecker took over Jake Brake in 1985, his mandate was unusually narrow.

**Most companies try to add improvement to the system. Danaher removed everything that competed with it.**

Koenigsaecker's job was not operations and improvement. It was improvement. Not as a side initiative. As the job. One person, one KPI, full bandwidth — accountable to nothing else.

Then they built a protocol around it. Every month, Shingijutsu consultants came in for a full week — not a quick visit, not a slide deck. A full week of structured kaizen: study the problem, make the change, confirm the result, move forward. Progress was tracked. Problems surfaced immediately. The cadence never slipped.

**That first night wasn't a breakthrough moment. It was the first loop of a repeatable system.**

---

## Why It Worked

### Six conditions. All structural. None motivational.

The initial change — reversing a cell layout — worked. So they expanded. One cell became many. Then the entire floor. Then other functions entirely. In finance, the same approach took the monthly accounting close from 25 people down to 9, with books closed in a day and a half instead of weeks.

**What made it work wasn't talent. It was design.**

Improvement had a single owner — one person whose primary job was moving the KPI, not a shared responsibility tacked onto an operations role. Time was protected from operational fires, scheduled and defended, not squeezed in after the urgent work was done. Progress was reviewed in tight weekly loops, so problems surfaced in week two instead of at the quarterly review. Changes were tested on one cell before committing to the floor — prove it in hours, then scale. Issues were escalated immediately, at the point they appeared, not downstream after three weeks of drift. And every action had one name and one date attached to it — no corridor agreements, no informal understandings that faded by Tuesday.

**None of this required extraordinary people. It required a system that made ordinary people consistently effective.**

---

## What Followed

### The system scaled. Because the structure scaled.

Jake Brake became more than a turnaround. It became the origin story of the Danaher Business System — the operating model Danaher would apply across every acquisition for the next four decades.

Inside Danaher, the story is still told deliberately, from one generation of leaders to the next. Because it captures a single idea that compounded into everything the company became: structural fixes outperform motivation.

The plant that once frustrated customers became consistent, reliable, and competitive. The cash it generated funded the next acquisition. And the next. From 1990 to 2023, Danaher grew earnings per share by roughly 10,000%.

**The system scaled. Because the structure scaled.**

---

## The Question to Take Back

Jake Brake isn't unique. What's rare is not the problem — it's the decision to fix it structurally.

Every company wants improvement. Very few design for it.

So the question isn't whether your business needs to improve. It does.

> Whose primary job is it to drive improvement in your business — every day, with protected time and clear accountability?

Not in theory. In your org chart.

**If the honest answer is "no one," you already know the constraint.**
