# My Process

## From "find a case study" to the finished newsletter

**Starting point — the research, not the writing.**

The assignment brief was clear that the case study had to demonstrate a specific mechanism, not just a general "Company X grew 30%" story. So the first step was finding a real company where the structural separation of improvement from operations was the actual turning point — not a side detail. Claude surfaced Jake Brake as the strongest candidate after evaluating three options. Amazon's Single-Threaded Owner model was the closest conceptual match to DeepThought's argument, but a tech giant's org design wouldn't resonate with a ₹200Cr manufacturer in Pune or Coimbatore. Danaher at scale had the same problem — too large to feel relevant. Jake Brake was the right fit: one struggling plant, one named person with one narrow mandate, a verifiable before-and-after, and a mechanism that maps directly to every condition in the brief.

**Drafting — iterative, not linear.**

The newsletter went through multiple rounds. The first Claude-generated draft was structurally sound but had two problems: the six conditions section was formatted as a list, which the brief explicitly forbids, and the opening was too explanatory — it told the reader what was happening rather than putting them in the scene. I rewrote both sections myself, tightening the opening and converting the conditions into a single flowing prose paragraph. I then ran the revised draft through ChatGPT to independently verify the argument structure and check whether the case study was serving the argument or just sitting alongside it. After that reran the entire draft through Gemini for any possible changes. The draft under went multiple cross-checks and rewrittings until at last, I brought it back to Claude for a final line-by-line audit against the brief's voice standards.

**The hardest editorial decision.**

The six conditions were the trickiest part. They're naturally list-shaped — six discrete items — but the brief is unambiguous: no bullet points in the newsletter body. The solution was to write them as a continuous prose paragraph with each condition named inline in bold. The structure stays visible, the reading rhythm doesn't break, and it forces the writing to connect each condition to the next rather than letting them sit as isolated items. That constraint made the section stronger.

**On AI hallucination.**

The main guardrail was source discipline. Every specific claim — the 11 p.m. walk, the counterclockwise cell, the 25-to-9 finance team, the kaizen cadence — was traced to a named primary source before it went into the newsletter. Where a number couldn't be verified (Jake Brake's plant-level revenue), it was cut and the gap disclosed in the footnotes. The multi-model workflow — Claude draft, my edits, ChatGPT verification, Claude final check — also helped catch anything one model might have accepted uncritically.
